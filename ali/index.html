🌱 First-year essentials

The single most important thing you can do this year: water deeply and consistently. Skip the fertilizer.

Watering schedule. Newly-planted root balls aren't connected to surrounding soil yet — they dry out fast and there's nowhere for roots to pull moisture from. Until those roots spread out (4–6 weeks), your watering routine is what keeps them alive.
  • First 2 weeks (now through early June): Water deeply every day. Soak the root zone — not a light spray. Aim for soil that's moist 4–6 inches down when you stick a finger in.
  • Mid-June through July: Every other day, deeper soaks.
  • August onward: Deep watering 2–3× per week unless it rains 1"+ that week.
Morning is best (less evaporation, leaves dry by night which prevents disease). If you only remember to water once a day, do it in the morning.

No fertilizer this year

Fertilizer pushes top growth that immature roots can't support. Let the plants put energy into roots first. You can start fertilizing in year 2 (and even then, most of these natives don't need much).

Mulch is doing half the work for you

Put down 2–3 inches of mulch (shredded bark, leaf mold, or compost) around all the plants — but keep it a few inches off the actual crowns and stems. This cuts watering needs roughly in half and suppresses weeds while plants are still small.

Minimal pruning

  • Pinch bee balm tips once in late May or early June (just snap off the top 2 inches) for a bushier, less leggy plant.
  • Deadhead bee balm and obedient plant after first bloom for a second flush.
  • Everything else: leave it alone, even after first bloom. Foliage and stems are still building the plant for next year.

Slug watch on new growth

Slugs love new soft growth on columbine, foamflower, polemonium, and bloodroot. If you see ragged holes appearing overnight, scatter iron phosphate bait (Sluggo or similar — it's pet-safe). Beer traps also work.

Don't panic if…

  • Bloodroot foliage vanishes in midsummer — it's a spring ephemeral. Goes dormant by July and comes back next March. Mark its spot so you don't dig it up.
  • Columbine looks ragged by late summer — cut foliage to the ground for fresh fall leaves.
  • Lupine flops in heat — first-year drama. Will be sturdier next year.
  • Things don't bloom heavily this year — perennials famously "sleep, creep, leap." Year 1 = sleep. Year 2 = creep. Year 3 = leap.

Before winter (October–November)

Top up mulch to 3–4 inches once the ground starts to cool. Leave standing stems and seed heads — they catch snow for insulation and feed birds. First-winter mortality on new plantings is normal — don't be alarmed if you lose one or two come spring.

The Plants

Bee Balm

Monarda didyma
☀️ Full–part sun 💧 Avg–high 📏 2–4 ft 🌸 Jul–Aug 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Easy 🌱 Year 1 🐝 Pollinator

Bright tubular flowers in red, pink, or purple that hummingbirds love. Aromatic minty leaves. Spreads by rhizomes — happily forms a patch.

Check-in signs

  • White powder on leaves → powdery mildew (the #1 bee balm issue)
  • Wilting in afternoon → thirsty; bee balm doesn't like to dry out
  • Tall floppy stems → didn't get pinched; will be fixed by year 2
  • Yellow lower leaves → normal late-summer aging, especially after mildew

Watch out for

  • Powdery mildew — improve airflow; water at base, not overhead
  • Spreading where you don't want it (controllable but vigorous)

Care notes

Pinch tips once in late May for bushier growth. Deadhead for a second flush. Cut to ground after frost — or leave 6" stubs as visual markers if reseeding is an issue. Year 1 watch: water consistently — first-year mildew often comes from drought stress.

Wild Lupine

Lupinus perennis
☀️ Full sun 💧 Low–avg 📏 1–2 ft 🌸 May–Jun 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Moderate 🌱 Year 1 🐝 Pollinator

Spires of blue-purple pea-like flowers above palmate leaves. The native host plant for the endangered Karner blue butterfly. Doesn't like to be moved — deep taproot.

Check-in signs

  • Yellowing leaves → too much water or poor drainage
  • Flopping in heat → first-year stress, will be sturdier in year 2
  • Aphids on flower spikes → spray off with water, usually harmless
  • Disappears after a few years → can be short-lived, often self-seeds replacements

Watch out for

  • Wet feet / root rot — needs well-drained soil
  • Aphids on bloom stalks

Care notes

Don't move once planted — deep taproot. Cut spent flower stems to tidy up; let some go to seed if you want it to spread. No fertilizer needed (it's a nitrogen-fixing legume). Year 1 watch: water deeply but let soil dry between waterings — lupine hates soggy roots.

Wild Columbine

Aquilegia canadensis
☀️ Part shade 💧 Average 📏 1–3 ft 🌸 May–Jun 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Easy 🌱 Year 1 🐝 Hummingbird favorite × 2

Nodding red-and-yellow flowers with distinctive long spurs that hummingbirds reach into. Delicate blue-green foliage. Short-lived (3–4 years) but self-seeds reliably.

Check-in signs

  • Squiggly white trails on leaves → leaf miners (cosmetic)
  • Ragged/yellowed foliage by late summer → normal; cut to ground for fresh leaves
  • Babies popping up nearby → self-seeded; transplant or leave as you like
  • Original plant disappears in year 3–4 → normal lifespan; the seedlings carry on

Watch out for

  • Leaf miners (cosmetic only)
  • Slugs on new spring growth

Care notes

Cut back the whole plant after bloom for a fresh flush of foliage. Let some seed heads ripen if you want self-sowing. Very low maintenance. Year 1 watch: slugs love new growth — bait if you see ragged holes.

False Indigo

Baptisia australis
☀️ Full sun 💧 Low 📏 3–4 ft 🌸 Late spring 🌎 NA Native 🛠️ Easy 🌱 Year 1 🐝 Pollinator

Shrub-like perennial with blue-purple flower spikes and black seed pods that rattle in winter wind. Deep taproot makes it nearly impossible to transplant — pick its spot once.

Check-in signs

  • No shoots by mid-May → normal, it's a late emerger
  • Floppy stems → first-year stress; may need light staking until established
  • Black seed pods late summer → ornamental, leave them through winter

Watch out for

  • Almost nothing — very pest- and disease-resistant

Care notes

Don't try to dig or move this once planted. Clear last year's dead stems in early spring (they snap off easily). No fertilizer needed — nitrogen-fixing legume. Year 1 watch: may look small and unimpressive this year — it's putting all its energy into the root system. Will explode in year 2–3.

Jacob's Ladder 'Hurricane'

Polemonium reptans 'Hurricane'
☀️ Part shade 💧 Average 📏 1–2 ft 🌸 May 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Easy 🌱 Year 1

A cultivar of the native creeping Jacob's Ladder with showy variegated foliage edged in cream, plus clusters of light blue spring flowers. The foliage is the year-round attraction.

Check-in signs

  • Leaf scorch (brown edges) → too much direct sun
  • Wilting in heat → needs more shade or more water
  • All-green leaves on some shoots → variegation reverting; cut those stems off at the base
  • Sparse/leggy → not enough light (variegated plants need more than all-green ones)

Watch out for

  • Variegation reversion — cut out any all-green shoots
  • Slugs on new growth

Care notes

Cut spent flower stems to tidy up. The foliage stays nice all season. Mulch to keep roots cool. Year 1 watch: if leaves scorch, the spot may be too sunny — note it for possible move next spring.

Lily of the Valley

Convallaria majalis
☀️ Part–full shade 💧 Average 📏 6–12 in 🌸 May 🌎 Europe ⚠️ Aggressive ⚠️ Toxic 🌱 Year 1

Heritage shade plant with fragrant nodding white bells in May. Treasured for the scent and tradition — but the only non-native here, and it has a serious dark side.

⚠️ All parts are toxic if eaten — cardiac glycosides, dangerous to pets and children. The red berries that form after bloom are especially tempting. Keep an eye on curious kids and dogs near this patch.
⚠️ Spreads aggressively via rhizomes — can easily overrun nearby plants. Will encroach on the foamflower, polemonium, and bloodroot patches if left unchecked. Plan to dig and divide the runners every 1–2 years, or contain with a buried barrier (root barrier or deep edging).

Check-in signs

  • Patch expanding noticeably each year → normal, will need containment
  • Yellowing leaves in late summer → normal dormancy onset
  • Red berries after bloom → toxic, remove if pets/kids are around
  • Encroaching on a neighbor plant → dig out the runners, root and all

Watch out for

  • Pets and children eating any part (including the berries)
  • Spreading into other plants' root zones
  • Hard to remove once established — be intentional about its footprint

Care notes

Each spring, walk the edge of the patch and pull any runners that have escaped into other plants. Cut spent flower stems. Leaves will yellow and disappear by August — that's normal. Year 1 watch: this is the year to set boundaries — note where it ends now so you can tell what's new spread next year.

Butterfly Milkweed

Asclepias tuberosa
☀️ Full sun 💧 Low 📏 1–2 ft 🌸 Jun–Aug 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Easy 🌱 Year 1 🦋 Monarch host

Brilliant orange flat-topped flower clusters above narrow leaves. The host plant for monarch butterfly caterpillars — chewed leaves are a feature, not a bug. Drought-tolerant once established.

Check-in signs

  • Chewed leaves with yellow-black-white striped caterpillars → MONARCHS! Leave them, this is the goal
  • Late to emerge (May) → normal, it's a late starter
  • Yellow leaves → too much water (loves dry feet)
  • Orange aphids in clusters → oleander aphid; spray off with water

Watch out for

  • Oleander aphids (the bright orange ones) — easily dislodged with a hose
  • Root rot in wet soil — drainage is essential

Care notes

Don't move it — deep taproot. Don't deadhead late in season — let seed pods form and split. Cut to ground after frost. Year 1 watch: won't get caterpillars right away while small — that's OK. Year 2+ will see monarchs.

Foamflower

Tiarella cordifolia
☀️ Part–full shade 💧 Avg–high 📏 6–12 in 🌸 Apr–Jun 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Easy 🌱 Year 1

Spreading shade-loving groundcover with maple-shaped leaves and frothy white bottlebrush flower spikes in spring. Spreads slowly by stolons to form a tidy patch.

Check-in signs

  • Crispy brown leaf edges → too much sun or too dry
  • Wilting → thirsty (loves consistent moisture)
  • Spreading runners → normal, this is how it forms a patch
  • Reddish tints on foliage in fall → normal seasonal color

Watch out for

  • Slugs on new growth
  • Drying out — needs more moisture than most natives

Care notes

Mulch to keep roots cool and moist. Cut spent flower spikes to tidy. Foliage is semi-evergreen — leave it through winter. Year 1 watch: this is one of the thirstier plants in the bed — check it more often than the drought-tolerant ones.

Obedient Plant

Physostegia virginiana
☀️ Full–part sun 💧 Avg–high 📏 3–4 ft 🌸 Aug–Sep 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Moderate 🌱 Year 1 ⚠️ Spreads

Tall pink flower spikes in late summer — individual flowers can be twisted on the stem and stay where you put them (hence "obedient"). Ironically, the plant itself spreads aggressively by rhizomes.

Check-in signs

  • Shoots popping up outside the original patch → normal spreading; pull or transplant runners
  • Flopping in bloom → first-year, or too rich/wet soil
  • Pale leaves → too much sun without enough water

Watch out for

  • Aggressive spread via underground runners
  • Powdery mildew in crowded conditions

Care notes

Each spring, pull runners that have escaped beyond the desired patch. Deadhead spent spikes to extend bloom. Cut to ground after frost. Year 1 watch: won't spread much this year — but mark where the original plant sits so you can identify and pull runners in year 2.

Spiderwort

Tradescantia virginiana
☀️ Full–part sun 💧 Average 📏 1–2 ft 🌸 May–Jul 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Easy 🌱 Year 1 🐝 Pollinator

Grassy strap-like leaves with three-petaled blue, purple, or pink flowers each morning (they close by afternoon). A new wave of flowers opens every morning for weeks.

Check-in signs

  • Tatty leaves by midsummer → normal; cut to the ground for fresh fall foliage
  • Self-seeded babies → can be vigorous; pull volunteers you don't want
  • Flowers closed in afternoon → totally normal (they open new each morning)

Watch out for

  • Self-seeding more than you want

Care notes

Cut whole plant down to 6" in midsummer for fresh foliage and possible rebloom. Very easy to divide and share. Year 1 watch: bloom should start this summer — enjoy the daily flower show.

Golden Alexander

Zizia aurea
☀️ Full–part sun 💧 Average 📏 1–3 ft 🌸 May–Jun 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Easy 🌱 Year 1 🦋 Swallowtail host

Bright yellow flat-topped flower clusters in late spring — important early-season nectar source. Host plant for black swallowtail butterfly caterpillars (similar leaves to parsley/dill).

Check-in signs

  • Striped green-and-yellow caterpillars on leaves → BLACK SWALLOWTAIL larvae! Leave them
  • Leaf damage with no caterpillars → check undersides; could be earwigs
  • Reddish fall color → normal late-season foliage
  • Seeds heavy after bloom → can self-seed mildly; pull volunteers if needed

Watch out for

  • Very little — tough native, mostly trouble-free

Care notes

Cut to ground in late fall, or leave standing for winter interest. Tolerates a wide range of soil. Year 1 watch: won't get caterpillars while small — but expect them in year 2 onward.

Bloodroot

Sanguinaria canadensis
☀️ Part–full shade 💧 Average 📏 6–10 in 🌸 Mar–Apr 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Easy 🌱 Year 1 ⚠️ Spring ephemeral

Pure white star-shaped flowers in earliest spring, often emerging before the distinctive lobed leaf unfurls. Foliage goes completely dormant by midsummer — the plant is alive underground, just resting.

⚠️ Foliage disappears by July. Bloodroot is a spring ephemeral — it does all its growing in the brief cool window of March–June, then goes dormant for the rest of the year. Mark the spot now so you don't dig it up by mistake later. It will be back next March.

Check-in signs

  • Leaves yellowing in June → start of normal summer dormancy, not death
  • Completely gone by August → totally normal, this is what ephemerals do
  • Single flower lasts only a few days → normal, very short bloom
  • Red-orange sap when stem is cut → that's where "bloodroot" comes from (skin irritant)

Watch out for

  • Accidentally digging it up when it's dormant in summer/fall — MARK the spot
  • Slugs on emerging spring growth
  • Toxic sap — wear gloves if dividing

Care notes

Plant or divide in early spring only (when leaves are visible). Mulch with leaf litter. Don't water in summer (it's dormant). Year 1 watch: place a small stake or plant marker where bloodroot sits so you know where it is when foliage disappears.
