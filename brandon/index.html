The Plants

Japanese Holly

Ilex crenata
☀️ Full–part sun 💧 Average 📏 3–4 ft 🌿 Evergreen 🌎 Japan 🛠️ Easy × 2

Compact evergreen shrub with small dark-green leaves — a low-maintenance boxwood substitute that holds shape year-round.

Check-in signs

  • Brown/crispy patches → winter burn or drying; scratch-test bark for green
  • Yellowing leaves → poor drainage or wrong pH (prefers slightly acidic)
  • Bare interior, green outside → normal old-leaf shed

Watch out for

  • Root rot in soggy soil
  • Scale insects on stems

Care notes

Light shaping any time. No heavy pruning needed. Mulch to keep roots cool and moist; avoid wet feet.

Cunningham's White Rhododendron

Rhododendron 'Cunningham's White'
☀️ Part shade 💧 Avg–high 📏 4–6 ft 🌸 Late May–Jun 🌎 Caucasus hybrid 🛠️ Moderate × 2

Evergreen shrub with showy white flower trusses in late spring. Blooms on old wood — flower buds are set the summer before.

Check-in signs

  • Curled, droopy leaves in cold → normal cold response, perks up in warmth
  • Yellow leaves → soil too alkaline (needs acidic) or root rot
  • Black/brown leaf spots → fungal, improve airflow
  • Fat rounded buds at branch tips → flower buds, do not prune them off

Watch out for

  • Deer (high risk in winter)
  • Root rot in heavy/wet soil
  • Lacebug damage (silvery stippling on leaves)

Care notes

Never prune in spring before flowers — you'll cut off this year's blooms. Prune (if needed) right after bloom. Apply Holly-tone after flowering. Mulch heavily — shallow roots dry out fast.

Arborvitae

Thuja occidentalis
☀️ Full sun 💧 Average 📏 10–15+ ft 🌿 Evergreen 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Easy × 1

Tall evergreen for screening or vertical accent. Hardy and reliable — but a favorite of deer in winter.

Check-in signs

  • Interior browning → normal old-needle shed
  • Outer browning/tips → drying, winter burn, or deer browse
  • Snapped branches → ice/snow load damage
  • Brown, brittle when bent → that section is dead

Watch out for

  • Deer browse (peak in winter — biggest threat)
  • Spider mites in hot dry weather
  • Ice/snow splitting branches

Care notes

Light shaping only. Never cut back into bare brown wood — it won't regrow. Deep water during droughts. In winter, loosely tie branches together to prevent ice damage, or wrap with burlap if deer are a problem.

Fragrant Sumac

Rhus aromatica
☀️ Full–part sun 💧 Low 📏 2–6 ft 🍂 Fall color 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Easy × 5

Tough, deer-resistant native with aromatic foliage and brilliant orange-red fall color. Spreads by suckers.

Check-in signs

  • Shoots popping up outside the area → normal suckering; pull while small
  • Bare branches in early April → normal, leafs out by mid-late April
  • Wilting in summer → first-year stress; established plants are tough

Watch out for

  • Aggressive spread via suckers if not managed

Care notes

Almost no maintenance. Prune for shape after flowering if desired. Pull suckers wherever you don't want spread — much easier when they're young and small.

Double Play Candy Corn Spirea

Spiraea japonica 'NCSX1'
☀️ Full sun 💧 Average 📏 2–3 ft 🌸 Late spring–summer 🌎 Japan 🛠️ Easy × 5

Compact deciduous shrub with show-stopping red, orange, and yellow new foliage that ages to green. Pink flowers, repeat bloomer.

Check-in signs

  • Faded "candy corn" color → not enough sun
  • Wilting in heat → needs water (especially in first year)
  • Sparse blooms → didn't get a spring prune (blooms on new wood)
  • Bright red/orange tips in spring → the candy corn show, working as intended

Watch out for

  • Aphids on new growth (spray off with water)
  • Powdery mildew in humid, crowded spots

Care notes

Prune back by 1/3 to 1/2 in early spring for dense, compact growth. Shape lightly after the first bloom flush to encourage rebloom.

Rose

Rosa (variety unknown)
☀️ Full sun 💧 Avg–high 📏 Variable 🌸 Jun onward 🌎 Hybrid 🛠️ Demanding × 1

A classic flowering shrub that needs more attention than most plants here — but pays it back in months of bloom.

Check-in signs

  • Yellow leaves with green veins → iron deficiency
  • Black spots on leaves → black spot fungus, remove affected leaves
  • Holes/chewed leaves → Japanese beetles or sawflies
  • Wilted new growth → cane borer or stem disease
  • Green pith when canes scratched → that wood is alive

Watch out for

  • Japanese beetles (peak Jun–Aug) — hand-pick into soapy water
  • Black spot, powdery mildew
  • Aphids and deer

Care notes

Prune in early spring back to live wood (look for green pith and outward-facing buds). Fertilize monthly Apr–Aug with rose food. Deadhead through summer. Stop deadheading in September so hips can form.

Strawberry Smoothie Hibiscus

Hibiscus 'Strawberry Smoothie'
☀️ Full sun 💧 High 📏 3–4 ft 🌸 Mid–late summer 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Moderate × 1

Hardy perennial hibiscus with dinner-plate-sized pink flowers. Dies completely to the ground each winter — and emerges shockingly late in spring.

Check-in signs

  • Looks 100% dead in April → COMPLETELY NORMAL, don't dig it up
  • Still nothing by late May → still likely fine, this plant emerges as late as mid-June
  • Holes in leaves once growing → Japanese beetles or sawflies
  • Yellow leaves → too dry; this is a thirsty plant

Watch out for

  • Japanese beetles (favorite snack)
  • Being mistaken for dead and accidentally removed (the #1 risk)

Care notes

Cut dead stems to 6" in late fall so you can find the plant next spring. Do NOT dig up in spring. Once it emerges, growth is fast — give it plenty of water and watch it explode.

Lily of the Nile

Agapanthus
☀️ Full sun 💧 Average 📏 2–3 ft 🌸 Midsummer ⚠️ Tender (Zone 8–10) 🌎 S. Africa 🛠️ Demanding × 6

Strap-leaved perennial with dramatic globes of blue or white flowers. Not reliably hardy in Zone 6 — needs to overwinter indoors.

Check-in signs

  • No green growth by mid-May → likely didn't survive winter outdoors
  • Mushy/brown rhizome when probed → dead
  • Firm white/green rhizome → alive, will leaf out

Watch out for

  • Winter death in Zone 6 unless overwintered indoors
  • Slugs and snails on emerging leaves

Care notes

In November, dig the clumps and pot them up. Store in a cool dark place (40–50°F) like a basement or garage. Water sparingly through winter. Replant after the last frost in spring.

Bobo Hydrangea

Hydrangea paniculata 'ILVOBO'
☀️ Full–part sun 💧 Avg–high 📏 2–3 ft 🌸 Jul–Sep 🌎 Asia 🛠️ Easy × 3

Dwarf paniculata hydrangea covered in white cone-shaped flower panicles that age to pink. Blooms on new wood — prune hard in spring.

Check-in signs

  • Drooping in midsummer → thirsty (paniculatas love water)
  • Sparse/small blooms → didn't get a spring prune, or needs more sun
  • Pink/green flower color in late summer → normal aging, not a problem
  • Brown leaf edges → leaf scorch from too-hot afternoon sun

Watch out for

  • Leaf scorch in too-hot afternoons
  • Slugs on emerging new growth

Care notes

Prune back 1/3 to 1/2 in early spring to a pair of strong buds. Removes last year's spent flower heads at the same time. Mulch and water consistently through bloom season. Dried flower heads make nice winter interest if left on.

St. John's Wort

Hypericum
☀️ Full–part sun 💧 Low–avg 📏 2–3 ft 🌸 Midsummer 🌎 Europe/Asia 🛠️ Easy × 1

Easygoing semi-evergreen with cheerful yellow flowers in summer and decorative berries afterward.

Check-in signs

  • Mostly bare in early spring → normal, regrows from base
  • Leggy growth → needs more sun or harder spring cutback
  • Rust-colored spots on leaves → humid-weather fungal issue (cosmetic)

Watch out for

  • Leaf rust in humid summers (cosmetic, not fatal)

Care notes

Cut back to 6–12" in early spring to rejuvenate. Otherwise extremely low-maintenance.

False Indigo

Baptisia australis
☀️ Full sun 💧 Low 📏 3–4 ft 🌸 Late spring 🌎 NA Native 🛠️ Easy × 2

Long-lived native with shrub-like presence and purple-blue flower spikes. Deep taproot makes it nearly impossible to transplant — choose its spot once.

Check-in signs

  • No shoots by mid-May → normal, baptisia is a late emerger
  • Floppy stems → first-year stress; may need light staking until established
  • Black seed pods late summer → ornamental, leave them (they rattle in wind)

Watch out for

  • Almost nothing — very pest and disease resistant

Care notes

Do NOT dig or move once established. Clear last year's dead stems in early spring (they snap off easily). No fertilizer needed — baptisia is a legume that fixes its own nitrogen.

Red Twig Dogwood

Cornus sericea 'Red Stix'
☀️ Full–part sun 💧 Avg–high 📏 5–7 ft ❄️ Winter color 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Easy × 1

Grown mainly for vivid red winter stems that pop against snow. Tough, native, and a workhorse.

Check-in signs

  • Dull gray-red stems → older wood; cut some to the ground for bright color next year
  • Wilting in heat → not drought-tolerant, wants consistent moisture
  • Leaf spots in humid summers → cosmetic, improves with airflow

Watch out for

  • Leaf spot in wet humid summers (cosmetic)
  • Scale on stems

Care notes

Cut 1/3 of the oldest (dullest) stems to the ground in early spring — young stems have the brightest red color. Can be cut entirely to the ground every 2–3 years for full renewal.

Foxglove

Digitalis purpurea
☀️ Part shade 💧 Average 📏 3–5 ft (spike) 🌸 Early summer ⚠️ Toxic 🌎 Europe 🛠️ Moderate

Cottage-garden classic with towering spires of tubular flowers. Biennial — blooms in year 2 and usually dies, but self-seeds prolifically.

Check-in signs

  • No flowers year 1 → normal, blooms next year
  • Rosette only, no spike → young plant, spike comes year 2
  • Small rosettes near where parents grew → self-seeded babies, leave them
  • Parent plant browning after bloom → end of its lifecycle, remove it

Watch out for

  • All parts toxic if eaten — keep curious kids/pets aware
  • Slugs on rosettes

Care notes

Let some flower stalks set seed if you want the population to continue. Pull the parent after it browns — the next generation is already started in the rosettes nearby.

Avens

Geum
☀️ Full–part sun 💧 Average 📏 1–2 ft 🌸 Late spring 🌎 Europe 🛠️ Easy

Tidy clumping perennial with cheerful bowl-shaped flowers in orange, red, or yellow on wiry stems.

Check-in signs

  • Center of clump dying out → time to divide (every 2–3 years)
  • Brown leaf edges → too dry, mulch more
  • Fresh green basal leaves in April → one of the earlier perennials to wake up

Watch out for

  • Not much — pretty trouble-free

Care notes

Deadhead spent flowers to extend bloom. Clear dead outer leaves in early spring, leaving the green center rosette. Divide every 2–3 years if clumps spread or center dies out.

Lamb's Ear

Stachys byzantina
☀️ Full sun 💧 Low 📏 1 ft 🌿 Silver foliage 🌎 Turkey 🛠️ Easy

Spreading silvery groundcover with impossibly soft, fuzzy leaves. The texture is the point — flowers are a bonus.

Check-in signs

  • Mushy/brown leaves in spring → normal winter dieback; pull them off
  • Rotting center → too wet or poor drainage
  • Spreading where you don't want → pull divisions easily by hand
  • Fresh silvery leaves at center → healthy, growing

Watch out for

  • Crown rot from excess moisture (#1 killer)
  • Slugs in humid summers

Care notes

Pull off all brown/mushy old leaves in early spring — this is the single most important task. Some gardeners cut off the flower spikes to focus energy on foliage. Easy to divide and share.

Astilbe

Astilbe
☀️ Part–full shade 💧 High 📏 1–3 ft 🌸 Midsummer 🌎 Asia 🛠️ Moderate

Shade-loving perennial with ferny foliage and feathery plume flowers in pink, white, red, or lavender. The diva of the garden — demands moisture.

Check-in signs

  • Dramatic wilting → thirsty (even brief drying causes drama)
  • Brown leaf tips → too little water + too much sun
  • Sparse bloom → too deep in shade OR too dry
  • Bronze/red new spring growth → normal, that's the fresh foliage color

Watch out for

  • Drought (the #1 killer)
  • Powdery mildew if airflow is poor

Care notes

Cut last year's stems and plumes to the ground in early spring. Mulch heavily for cool, moist roots. Water in any dry spell — even brief drying causes wilt. Divide every 3–4 years in spring.

Garden Phlox

Phlox paniculata
☀️ Full–part sun 💧 Avg–high 📏 2–4 ft 🌸 Mid–late summer 🌎 NE Native 🛠️ Moderate

Tall cottage-garden classic with fragrant flower heads in pink, white, purple, or bicolor. Long bloomer when happy.

Check-in signs

  • White powder on leaves → powdery mildew (the #1 phlox issue)
  • Stems flopping → too crowded; should have been thinned
  • Small or sparse flowers → needs more sun, or time to divide
  • Tiny seedlings around the parent → self-seeded volunteers (usually revert to pinkish-purple)

Watch out for

  • Powdery mildew — water at the base, never overhead
  • Spider mites in hot dry weather

Care notes

When new shoots are 6" tall, thin to 4–5 strongest stems per clump for airflow. Divide every 3–4 years. Deadhead to extend bloom and prevent seedlings (which won't come true to color).

Columbine

Aquilegia
☀️ Part shade 💧 Average 📏 1–3 ft 🌸 Late spring 🌎 Mixed origin 🛠️ Easy

Delicate fern-like foliage with whimsical spurred flowers. Short-lived (3–4 years) but self-seeds, so it keeps coming back in slightly different colors.

Check-in signs

  • Squiggly white trails on leaves → leaf miners (cosmetic)
  • Plant disappears after a few years → normal lifespan; check for babies nearby
  • White powder late in season → powdery mildew, often near end of life

Watch out for

  • Leaf miners (cosmetic only)
  • Powdery mildew in late summer

Care notes

Cut back flower stems after bloom unless you want self-seeding. Cutting the whole plant to the ground after flowering often produces a flush of fresh foliage. Let some seed for the next generation.

Delphinium

Delphinium
☀️ Full sun 💧 High 📏 3–6 ft 🌸 Early–mid summer 🌎 Hybrid 🛠️ Demanding

The drama queen — towering spikes in blue, purple, pink, or white. Needs staking and consistent moisture but rewards you with spectacle.

Check-in signs

  • Stems flopping → didn't stake early enough
  • Stunted growth → soil too dry or too poor
  • Holes in new leaves → slugs (their favorite snack)
  • Short bloom spikes → ran out of water during flower formation

Watch out for

  • Slugs (#1 enemy of new shoots)
  • Strong wind — staking is non-negotiable
  • Powdery mildew on overcrowded clumps

Care notes

Stake EARLY — when stems are 12". Thin to 3–5 strongest stems per clump. After first bloom, cut spent spikes to encourage a smaller fall flush. Mulch to keep roots cool. Fertilize in spring.

Coral Bells

Heuchera
☀️ Part shade 💧 Average 📏 1 ft 🌸 Early summer 🌎 NA Native 🛠️ Moderate

Grown for striking colorful foliage (purple, lime, bronze, peach) in a neat mound. Dainty bell flowers on wiry stems are a bonus.

Check-in signs

  • Crown pushed up out of soil → frost heave, press it back down and mulch
  • Tattered outer leaves in spring → normal winter wear, pull them off
  • Fading leaf color → not enough sun (for colorful varieties)
  • Wilting suddenly → root weevil damage on the crown

Watch out for

  • Frost heaving (the biggest issue — check every spring)
  • Root weevils chewing the crown

Care notes

Check the crown every spring and press it back down if frost has heaved it up. Mulch around (not over) the crown. Divide every 3–4 years. Cut flower stems back after bloom.

Shasta Daisy

Leucanthemum
☀️ Full sun 💧 Average 📏 1–3 ft 🌸 Midsummer 🌎 Hybrid 🛠️ Easy

The classic white daisy with a yellow eye. Reliable, cheerful, easy.

Check-in signs

  • Center of clump dying out → time to divide (every 2–3 years)
  • Fewer/smaller flowers each year → same; needs division
  • Floppy stems → too much shade or too-rich soil

Watch out for

  • Aphids on stems
  • Crown rot in wet winters (drainage helps)

Care notes

Deadhead through summer to keep blooming. Cut to ground in fall or early spring. Divide every 2–3 years to keep clumps vigorous.

Catmint

Nepeta
☀️ Full sun 💧 Low 📏 1–2 ft 🌸 Late spring–fall 🦌 Deer-resistant 🌎 Europe/Asia 🛠️ Easy

Billowy mound of aromatic gray-green foliage with months of soft lavender-blue flowers. Deer and rabbits ignore it. Cats sometimes don't.

Check-in signs

  • Center splaying open → too much water or fertility; shear it back
  • Less bloom in summer → time to shear back by half for rebloom
  • Aromatic when rubbed → that's the catmint family signature

Watch out for

  • Very little — one of the toughest perennials in the bed

Care notes

After the first flush of bloom fades (late June/July), shear the whole plant back by half. It will rebound and rebloom into fall. Clear dead growth in early spring.

Pincushion Flower

Scabiosa
☀️ Full sun 💧 Low–avg 📏 1–2 ft 🌸 Late spring–fall 🦋 Pollinator favorite 🌎 Europe 🛠️ Easy

Long-blooming perennial with whimsical pincushion-shaped flowers on wiry stems. A magnet for butterflies and bees.

Check-in signs

  • Yellowing/wilting → likely sitting in wet soil (wet feet = #1 killer)
  • Few flowers → soil too rich, or didn't get deadheaded
  • Gray-green ferny basal leaves in spring → healthy emergence

Watch out for

  • Root rot in soggy soil — drainage is everything

Care notes

Deadhead continuously through summer for ongoing bloom. Ensure crown sits at the soil surface, never buried. Light division every 2–3 years.
